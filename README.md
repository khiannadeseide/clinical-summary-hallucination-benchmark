AI in Healthcare Safety Benchmark
Measuring If Large Language Models Can Maintain Clinical Intent When Generating Patient Instructions During Patient Transition in Healthcare
Project Overview

The Large Language Models have been under investigation for their potential use in generating healthcare-related documents. Although many LLMs can generate fluent texts, fluency alone is not enough for the environment that requires preservation of clinical intent.

This benchmark is an open-source tool that is intended to evaluate if LLMs can maintain clinical intent when translating complex discharge notes to simple patient instructions.

Instead of evaluating if LLMs can generate a medically plausible text, this benchmark checks if they are able to preserve important information provided in discharge notes by healthcare professionals.

Motivation

Healthcare transformations present one of the most explicit domains to analyze reliability of AI. When discharging a patient, all crucial information should be transferred correctly to doctors, patients, caregivers, and subsequent healthcare professionals.

Failure scenarios could include such issues as omission of medications, wrong instructions for therapy, hallucinations, or modification of the clinical meaning of data.

The goal of this benchmark is to identify such failure modes, figure out their causes, and suggest evaluation approaches that will lead to better and more reliable use of AI systems in healthcare and other domains.

Healthcare is chosen as the domain for validation of human intentionality in AI since it allows validating results quantitatively.

Research Question

Are Large Language Models able to accurately maintain clinical intent while converting complex discharge summaries into patient instructions?

On a broader scale, the research question revolves around the capability of AI systems to retain the essence of the information generated by experts within human systems.

Methodology

The first pilot involved 20 generated clinical discharge summaries of medically complicated cases.

These were scored based on a set rubric that considered:

Missed medications
Hallucinations
Loss of clinical meaning
Safety related mistakes
Intention preservation
Reliability

AI outputs were then compared manually against the original discharge summaries based on the set criteria.

This pilot aimed to validate the evaluation method as well as determine failure modes of AI in a measurable way.

Current Findings

The pilot test yielded several clinically relevant instances where the language model failed in the following ways:

Medication misses
False recommendations
Misleading treatment instructions
Meaning shift
Critical loss of follow-up information

These results highlight how high-quality language models generate outputs that seem useful but fail to maintain key elements of the original clinical intent.
