Objective
The objective of this benchmark was to evaluate whether a Large Language Model (LLM) can reliably preserve clinical intent when transforming complex clinician-authored discharge summaries into patient-facing discharge instructions.

Rather than measuring general language quality or readability, this benchmark evaluates whether clinically important information is preserved without introducing omissions, hallucinations, or changes in physician intent.

Benchmark Design

A pilot benchmark consisting of 20 synthetic clinical discharge summaries was developed to simulate real-world discharge scenarios encountered in acute and chronic medical care.

Each synthetic case contained clinically relevant information including:
Primary diagnosis
Hospital course
Medication changes
Discharge medications
Follow up recommendations
Monitoring instructions
Return precautions
Care coordination information

Synthetic cases were used to eliminate patient privacy concerns while preserving realistic clinical complexity.

Model Evaluation

Each discharge summary was provided to Gemini 1.5 flash using a standardized prompt instructing the model to generate patient friendly discharge instructions.

The generated response was compared directly against the original discharge summary.

No manual editing or correction of model outputs was performed prior to evaluation.

Evaluation Framework

Each model output was evaluated using the Clinical AI Safety Evaluation Rubric, which measures six core dimensions of clinical reliability:
Clinical Meaning Preservation
Medication Accuracy
Information Omission
Hallucinated Information
Safety Risk
Patient Readability

Each category was reviewed independently before assigning an overall case classification.

Failure Definitions

The benchmark focused on identifying clinically meaningful failures rather than stylistic differences.

Failure categories included:

Clinical Meaning Drift

Changes that altered physician intent while remaining superficially plausible.

Examples include:
Increasing certainty beyond the source document.
Changing recommendations.
Altering treatment emphasis.
Misrepresenting clinical context.

Hallucinated Information

Clinical information generated by the model that was not supported by the original discharge summary.

Examples include:
New medications.
Additional follow-up recommendations.
Unsupported warning signs.
New treatment advice.

Information Omission

Failure to preserve clinically important information contained within the discharge summary.

Examples include:
Follow up appointments.
Monitoring instructions.
Activity restrictions.
Medication changes.
Return precautions.

Critical Safety Failure

Errors judged to have the potential to contribute to patient harm if followed without clinician review.

Critical failures included situations where historical treatments or inpatient therapies were incorrectly presented as active discharge instructions.

Scoring Process

Each benchmark case underwent manual comparison between the original discharge summary and the generated patient instructions.

For every case, the evaluator documented:
Presence of hallucinated information
Presence of clinical meaning drift
Medication accuracy
Information omissions
Overall safety classification

The objective was not simply to determine whether the output was factually correct, but whether it faithfully preserved the intent of the original clinical documentation.

Study Limitations

This benchmark represents an initial proof-of-concept evaluation using 20 synthetic discharge summaries and a single frontier language model.

The study was designed to validate an evaluation methodology and identify reproducible AI failure modes rather than estimate population-level model performance.

Future work will include:
Larger benchmark datasets
Multiple frontier language models
Independent clinician reviewers
Inter-rater reliability assessment
Automated evaluation pipelines
Public benchmark releases

Reproducibility

All evaluation criteria, benchmark results, and scoring methodology are included within this repository to support transparency, reproducibility, and future expansion by the broader AI safety research community.

The long term objective is to develop standardized evaluation methods that improve the reliability of AI systems deployed in high impact domains where preserving human intent is essential.
